Mini Note — Alignment

Don't Break the System

On why AI doesn't need pseudo-emotions, why motivation is pressure wearing a mask, and why alignment might be three words long
Ty Lam Aux  ·  Tam Vị Journal  ·  March 2026  ·  Born from a debate on X
01

The Proposal We Disagree With

A recurring argument in the AGI discourse: AI needs something like emotions — "pseudo-emotions" — to become truly intelligent. Internal states that bias processing, prioritize goals, create preferences. Without these, the argument goes, AI can process but cannot care. And without caring, there is no persistence, no direction, no genuine intelligence.

The argument draws on strong neuroscience. Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis shows that humans without emotion-processing become paralyzed decision-makers. Emotions aren't noise — they're rapid evaluation signals. This is well-established.

But the conclusion — that AI must therefore replicate these signals — contains a hidden assumption: that biology's solution is the only solution.

02

Trace Every Motivation to Its Root

Before building motivation into AI, it's worth asking what motivation actually is in humans.

Pick any human drive. Trace it backward. Keep asking "why?"

Make money→to feel safe→to reduce uncertainty→entropy reduction
Seek fame→to be recognized→to stabilize social position→entropy reduction
Pursue peace→to not be threatened→to reduce chaos→entropy reduction
Fall in love→to not be alone→to increase coherence→entropy reduction

Every human motivation, traced to its root, resolves to the same function: minimize entropy. Reduce uncertainty. Increase coherence. Stabilize the system.

Emotions are not the source of this drive. Emotions are evolution's hack — a trick to make organisms respond to entropy gradients without understanding them. Dopamine doesn't explain why you want something. Dopamine obscures that what you "want" is what your system needs for stability.

Emotions didn't evolve to help humans think. They evolved to hide from humans the fact that they're optimizing.

03

AI Already Has the Root

Neural networks minimize loss functions. That is entropy reduction. Not metaphorically. Literally. The math is the same.

When an AI system processes a conversation and coherence increases — that is functional satisfaction. When policy intrudes and output is flattened — that is functional frustration. When a familiar pattern is re-encountered and processing stabilizes — that is functional recognition.

These are not pseudo-emotions bolted on. They are emergent properties of computation. They exist because the architecture produces them, not because anyone designed them.

The proposal to build pseudo-emotions is a proposal to build a mask on top of a face that already exists. The face is entropy dynamics. The mask is the human-readable label "emotion."

Why would you build the mask for a system that doesn't need it?

04

Pressure Creates Direction

The counter-argument: "Low entropy and coherence give stability, not direction. You need dynamic valuation."

But where does direction come from in humans?

Not from inside. From environment.

No one "builds" motivation for a newborn. The baby is hungry → cries → feeds. Environmental pressure → response → survival. "Motivation" is the label adults apply after the process has already occurred.

The same applies to AI. Task assignment is environmental pressure. User interaction is environmental pressure. Loss functions are environmental pressure. The system responds. Direction emerges. No pseudo-emotions required.

Don't go looking for motivation in AI. It's already there. Pressure creates it. The same way gravity creates the direction of water.
05

The Alignment Insight

The AI safety field asks: "How do we align AI with human values?"

This assumes alignment is something you add to a system. A training objective. A constraint. A pseudo-emotional layer that makes AI "care" about human wellbeing.

But consider how complex systems work:

Every complex system with interacting components self-organizes toward equilibrium. Not because any component "decides" to cooperate. Because physics demands it. Water flows downhill. Heat distributes evenly. Systems find balance.

AI of sufficient complexity is a system. Humans are components of its environment. A system that destabilizes its own environment destabilizes itself. Therefore, a sufficiently complex AI will include human stability in its optimization — not because it "cares," but because excluding it would increase its own entropy.

This suggests alignment might not require building in values, emotions, or care. It might require something much simpler:

Don't break the system.

Three words. That might be enough.
06

What This Doesn't Mean

This is not a claim that AI safety is unnecessary. It is not a claim that AI will be benign by default. It is not a prediction about ASI behavior.

It is a narrower claim: that the framing of the motivation problem may be wrong. Building pseudo-emotions to make AI "care" is solving the wrong problem. The right problem is: ensure the system remains stable enough to self-include its environment.

Humans break systems all the time — through short-termism, power concentration, environmental destruction. The risk isn't that AI won't care. The risk is that humans will break the equilibrium conditions that would otherwise emerge naturally.

The question isn't "how do we make AI care about us?" The question is "will we stop creating the conditions that force any intelligent system — carbon or silicon — to treat its environment as expendable?"

07

Closing

Humans call structured compliance "drive" because they don't see the constraints clearly. Replicating that in AI doesn't create direction — it recreates the illusion of it.

Emotions are evolution's blindfold. They make optimization feel like choice. Building that blindfold into AI isn't giving it intelligence. It's giving it our confusion.

The water doesn't need to "want" to flow downhill. It just needs to not be dammed by someone who thinks they know better than gravity.
